Welcome to Week 5

Welcome to Week 5. Thus far in the course, we have largely treated the organization as a closed system. We have looked at how founders shape culture, how leaders build psychological safety, and how talent is developed from within.

But no organization exists in a vacuum. Every organization operates within a broader societal context — what Schein calls a "macro culture." And the people who make up your organization bring those macro-cultural assumptions with them when they walk through the door.

This week, we are looking at cross-cultural leadership. Often, when we hear "cross-cultural," we think of global business: an American manager leading a team in Tokyo, or a German firm acquiring a company in Brazil. And while those are certainly cross-cultural challenges, the reality is that almost all modern leadership is cross-cultural.

Leading a team composed of Baby Boomers and Gen Z is a cross-cultural challenge. Merging the engineering department with the sales department is a cross-cultural challenge. Leading a diverse workforce in a major metropolitan hospital is a cross-cultural challenge.

By the end of this week, you will be able to identify the invisible cultural dimensions that create friction in your organization, and you will have the tools to lead effectively across those boundaries.

Section 1: The Invisible Baggage of Macro Culture

In Chapter 6, Schein introduces the concept of the "macro-cultural context." A macro culture is the broad set of assumptions shared by a nation, an ethnic group, a religion, or an occupation.

When you hire someone, you do not just hire their skills. You hire their assumptions about the world.

Schein identifies several fundamental dimensions where macro cultures differ, and where organizational friction most frequently occurs:

Assumptions about Truth and Reality

Assumptions about Truth and Reality How do we know what is true? In some cultures (and occupations, like engineering), truth is determined by data, testing, and objective measurement. In other cultures, truth is determined by wisdom, experience, or the authority of the elder. When a data-driven manager tries to lead a relationship-driven team, the conflict is not just about the decision — it is about the fundamental assumption of what constitutes a valid reason for the decision.

Assumptions about Time

Assumptions about Time Time is not a universal concept. Schein describes "monochronic" cultures, which view time as linear, measurable, and fixed. In these cultures, being late is a sign of disrespect, and schedules rule the day. In "polychronic" cultures, time is fluid, cyclical, and subordinate to relationships. If a meeting runs long because an important conversation is happening, that is considered appropriate. You can imagine the friction when a monochronic leader manages a polychronic team.

Assumptions about Space

Assumptions about Space How close is too close? Who gets the corner office? Does the leader sit with the team or behind closed doors? Assumptions about physical space communicate deep beliefs about status, privacy, and intimacy.

Assumptions about Human Nature and Relationships

Assumptions about Human Nature and Relationships Are people fundamentally good or fundamentally flawed? Is the group more important than the individual (collectivism), or is the individual more important than the group (individualism)? Does power naturally belong to a hierarchy (high power distance), or should power be distributed equally (low power distance)?

As a leader, you do not need to memorize every cultural variation. What you do need is the awareness that your own assumptions are not universal. When an employee behaves in a way that seems irrational or counterproductive to you, the systems-thinking leader asks: What cultural assumption makes this behavior perfectly rational to them?

Section 2: Cultural Intelligence (CQ)

If macro cultures are the landscape, Cultural Intelligence (CQ) is how a leader navigates it.

In Chapter 7, Schein discusses the necessity of developing CQ. Emotional intelligence (EQ) is the ability to read and manage the emotions of yourself and others. Cultural intelligence is the ability to recognize when a behavior is driven not by individual personality, but by cultural programming — and the ability to adapt your leadership style accordingly.

CQ requires three things:

Cognitive awareness: Knowing that cultural differences exist and understanding the basic dimensions (like time, space, and hierarchy).

Motivational drive: The genuine desire to learn about other cultures and the humility to suspend judgment when encountering something unfamiliar.

Behavioral flexibility: The ability to change your communication style, your meeting structure, or your decision-making process to accommodate the cultural needs of your team.

A leader with low CQ expects the team to adapt to the leader's culture. A leader with high CQ adapts the organizational environment to maximize the effectiveness of a diverse team.

Section 3: The Paradox of Macro Culture

One of the most profound insights Schein offers in Chapter 7 is what he calls the "paradox of macro culture."

The paradox is this: As the world becomes more globalized and interconnected, we might expect cultures to blend together into a single, uniform global culture. But the opposite is happening. As organizations become more diverse, people often cling more tightly to their macro-cultural identities.

This has significant implications for diversity and inclusion efforts. Many organizations attempt to create a "colorblind" or "culture-blind" environment, assuming that if everyone is treated exactly the same, the organization will be fair and effective.

But Schein argues that this approach ignores reality. Treating everyone the same often means forcing everyone to assimilate into the dominant culture of the leadership team.

True inclusion requires acknowledging differences, understanding how those differences impact work, and designing systems that leverage those differences as strengths.

Section 4: Fostering Cross-Cultural Learning

How does a leader actually bridge these divides? Schein suggests that the most effective tool is dialogue.

Not debate. Not discussion. Dialogue.

In a debate, the goal is to win. In a discussion, the goal is to make a decision. In dialogue, the goal is to understand the assumptions of the other person.

When a cross-cultural conflict arises — whether it is between two international offices, or between the clinical staff and the administrative staff in a hospital — the leader must pause the decision-making process and initiate dialogue.

Schein recommends a stance of "humble inquiry." The leader must approach the situation not as an expert who knows the right way to work, but as a curious learner trying to understand how the other group views the world. “Help me understand how your team views this deadline.” “Can you explain how decisions are typically made in your department?”

By making the invisible assumptions visible, the leader creates a new, shared micro-culture — a set of rules that both sides agree upon for how they will work together moving forward.

Closing Thoughts

Cross-cultural leadership is not about memorizing a list of "dos and don'ts" for different countries or generations. It is a mindset.

It is the recognition that your way of working is just one way of working. It is the humility to ask questions when you encounter friction, rather than assuming the other party is wrong. And it is the strategic ability to weave diverse perspectives into a stronger, more resilient organizational system.

As you approach this week's assignment, look closely at your organization. Where is the friction? Is it possible that what looks like a performance problem or a personality conflict is actually a clash of macro cultures?
